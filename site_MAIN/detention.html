Military Court Architecture

The three parallel legal instruments enabling indefinite detention without charge or trial, governing Palestinians across the West Bank, Gaza Strip, and inside Israel's 1948 boundaries. No equivalent applies to Israeli citizens in the same territories.

Instrument I
Military Order 1651

The primary instrument governing the West Bank. Article 285 empowers military commanders to detain civilians for renewable six-month periods on undisclosed "security" grounds with no statutory cap on renewals. Orders are reviewed in closed hearings using secret evidence that neither the defendant nor their lawyer is permitted to see. The UN Working Group on Arbitrary Detention assessed Military Order 1651 as a de jure violation of Article 9(3) of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights.

Read Legal Analysis
Instrument II
Emergency Powers (Detention) Law

Applies inside Israel's 1948 boundaries, enabling detention without trial for Palestinian citizens of Israel and residents of East Jerusalem. Enacted in 1979 under emergency legislation, it has been renewed by the Knesset annually for over four decades without interruption, normalising indefinite incarceration as standard peacetime practice. Amnesty International describes its use as "administrative detention by another name."

Read Amnesty Report
Instrument III
Unlawful Combatants Law

Deployed extensively post-October 7, 2023, to classify Gaza detainees as neither civilians under the Fourth Geneva Convention nor prisoners of war under the Third, creating a deliberate legal void. Detainees processed under this law at facilities such as Sde Teiman were held without access to lawyers, ICRC inspectors, or family contact. OHCHR assessed conditions at these facilities as "consistent with torture."

MILITARY COURT BENCHMARK — IDF ANNUAL REPORT VIA HAARETZ
99.74% Conviction Rate

In 2010, out of 9,542 cases processed through Israeli military courts, only 25 ended in full acquittals. Between 2005 and 2010, 835 Palestinian minors were charged with stone-throwing in Israeli military courts — one was acquitted. Proceedings typically last under 10 minutes. Judges and prosecutors wear identical IDF uniforms. Defendants have no right to confront secret evidence. In 2006, the conviction rate was 99.71% (26 acquittals out of 8,854 cases), confirming the pattern is structural, not incidental. The US State Department's 2013 Human Rights Report confirmed Palestinians in Israeli military courts were convicted more than 99% of the time.

PHRI REPORT — NOVEMBER 2025

Physicians for Human Rights – Israel documented that fewer than 30 Palestinians died in Israeli custody during the entire decade preceding the October 7 war. Since then, the mortality rate has averaged one death every four days. PHRI attributes deaths to a combination of physical violence, deliberate medical neglect, and systematic abuse, characterising the situation as "systematic killings and cover-ups." Under National Security Minister Itamar Ben-Gvir, detention facilities have "effectively turned into venues of torture and maltreatment." Israeli authorities last issued official detention death figures in September 2024; PHRI researchers subsequently identified at least 35 additional deaths beyond that cutoff, which Israeli authorities confirmed when pressed.

Notable Long-Term Detainees

High-profile Palestinian political figures held under long-term sentences or administrative orders, without recognition of prisoner-of-war status under the Third Geneva Convention.

Detainee File 001
Marwan Barghouti

Fatah leader and the most prominent Palestinian political prisoner, detained since 2002 and serving five life sentences plus 40 years. Since October 7, 2023, he has been held in solitary confinement. His lawyer reported that he was denied medical treatment for injuries sustained in beatings. Barghouti consistently polls as the most popular figure among Palestinians for a future political leadership role and was included in Hamas's priority list for prisoner exchange negotiations.

Read Le Monde Report
Detainee File 002
Ahmad Saadat

General Secretary of the Popular Front for the Liberation of Palestine (PFLP) for more than 24 years. Serving a 30-year sentence after being forcibly transferred from a Palestinian Authority-administered prison in Jericho by Israeli forces in 2006. Human rights organisations classify his prosecution as politically motivated. He was included on Hamas's prisoner exchange priority list and his case has been raised in multiple UN Special Procedures reports on arbitrary detention.

Read Middle East Eye Report
Detainee File 003
Child Administrative Detainees

As of 2025, children under administrative detention represent 37% of all Palestinian child detainees — up from 15% the previous year, a near-fourfold increase following October 7. Approximately 700 Palestinian children between ages 12 and 17 are processed through the Israeli military detention system each year. UNICEF confirmed the treatment of Palestinian minors in this system is "widespread, systematic and institutionalised," meeting the threshold for cruel, inhuman and degrading treatment under the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child.

Interrogation & Protocol Logs

Declassified intelligence manuals and whistleblower testimonies regarding systematic violations of the UN Convention Against Torture (UNCAT).

Protocol 01
Stress Positions

Documentation of "Shabeh" (prolonged binding in contorted positions), sleep deprivation, and sensory bombardment used systematically during interrogations to extract forced confessions.

Read Addameer Report
Protocol 02
Medical Negligence

Logs detailing the deliberate withholding of critical medication and treatment for chronic illnesses as a punitive measure against political prisoners and administrative detainees.

Read Addameer Report
Protocol 03
Solitary Confinement

Declassified reports tracking the psychological torture of prolonged isolation, specifically targeting minors to coerce confessions without the presence of legal counsel or parents.

Read DCI-Palestine Report
Protocol 04
Incommunicado Detention

Directives enforcing the complete severance of prisoner contact with the outside world, explicitly banning ICRC (Red Cross) visitations, legal representation, and family phone calls.

Read ICRC Access Report
Protocol 05
Pre-Dawn Raids & Night Arrests

Documented pattern of IDF units conducting arrests between 02:00–04:00 using psychological shock tactics on households with children present. Detainees are blindfolded, zip-tied, and transferred directly to military vehicles without identity confirmation, attorney notification, or charge documentation. DCI-Palestine has recorded this tactic in the majority of child arrest cases it has documented since 2012.

Read DCI-Palestine Report
Protocol 06
Hebrew-Only Confessions

Testimony compiled by Military Court Watch documenting the routine presentation of confession documents to Palestinian detainees — including minors — exclusively in Hebrew, a language the vast majority of defendants cannot read or understand. Signed confessions form the evidentiary basis of prosecution in over 80% of plea-bargained military court cases, making translation denial a structural instrument of conviction rather than an administrative failure.

Read Military Court Watch Report
Protocol 07
Sexual Violence in Detention

The UN Special Rapporteur on Torture and OHCHR documented credible, corroborated allegations of sexual assault, forced nudity, genital targeting, and rape used against Palestinian detainees at Sde Teiman and other post-October 7 facilities. OHCHR assessed these acts as potentially constituting war crimes and crimes against humanity under the Rome Statute and customary international humanitarian law. Israel's own Knesset briefly launched a probe before it was suppressed under pressure.

Read OHCHR Statement
Protocol 08
Denial of Legal Counsel

UNICEF and Addameer confirm Palestinian children are systematically denied access to legal representation during interrogation. Under Military Order 1651, prosecutors may withhold all evidence from defence lawyers on "security" grounds without limit. In West Bank military courts, attorneys are routinely given fewer than 24 hours' notice before hearings, access to case files is frequently denied entirely, and hearings proceed regardless. The result is a system in which legal representation is formally permitted but structurally non-functional.
