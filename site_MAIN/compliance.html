State Compliance Monitor

Tracking Global Adherence to ICJ Provisional Orders

Verified logs of third-state actions following the ICJ rulings on the prevention of genocide and the legality of the occupation.

THIRD-STATE OBLIGATIONSLEGAL FRAMEWORK
Under Common Article 1 of the Geneva Conventions and the Genocide Convention, all third-party states have a positive obligation to ensure respect for international humanitarian law. This includes the cessation of diplomatic cover, halting arms transfers that risk being used in war crimes, and fully complying with ICJ and ICC mandates.
ORD-01
Arms Embargo

The Netherlands: F-35 Export Halt

Hague Court of Appeals ordered the suspension of all exports of F-35 parts, citing a clear risk of serious violations of international humanitarian law. Dutch Foreign Minister Caspar Veldkamp subsequently cancelled a planned visit to Israel and confirmed to parliament that Netanyahu would be subject to arrest if he entered the Netherlands following the ICC warrant.

Diplomatic Status

Spain: Recognition of Statehood

Official recognition of the State of Palestine on May 28, 2024, alongside Ireland and Norway — aligning with international legal consensus on the right to self-determination. Israel withdrew its ambassador from Madrid in response. Spain also suspended its arms export licenses to Israel, and Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez called on other EU members to follow.

ORD-03
Legal Intervention

South Africa: ICJ Application

Filing of proceedings at the International Court of Justice invoking the Genocide Convention on December 29, 2023 — prompting the issuance of provisional measures on January 26, 2024 ordering Israel to take all measures to prevent genocidal acts and to preserve evidence. Oral hearings resumed in February 2024 with third-party interventions from Nicaragua, Mexico, Colombia, and others.

ORD-04
Diplomatic Severance

Colombia: Severing Ties

Formal severing of diplomatic relations by President Gustavo Petro, citing the ongoing military assault and non-compliance with UN Security Council ceasefire demands. Colombia also halted coal exports to Israel and submitted a declaration of intervention in the South Africa v. Israel ICJ case, directly supporting the genocide application.

ORD-05
Trade Embargo

Turkey: Trade Halt

Complete suspension of all export and import trade operations with Israel, conditional upon the realization of uninterrupted and adequate humanitarian aid flows into Gaza. Turkey's Ministry of Trade confirmed the suspension covers 54 product categories. Turkey also supported South Africa's ICJ genocide proceedings and referred Israel to the ICC.

View Trade Order
ORD-06
Partial Arms Halt

United Kingdom: License Suspension

Suspension of 30 out of 350 arms export licenses in September 2024, following a government legal review identifying a clear risk of components being used to violate International Humanitarian Law. Canada, Australia, and Japan's Itochu Corporation were cited by OHCHR as parallel cases warranting full embargo escalation. No further licenses were issued for components assessed as likely deployed in Gaza.

ORD-07
Judicial Support

France: ICC Independence Reaffirmation

Following the November 21, 2024 ICC arrest warrants for Netanyahu and Gallant, France initially signalled it would comply with its Article 89 Rome Statute obligations. The French Foreign Ministry subsequently qualified this by citing "immunities applicable to heads of state not party to the ICC" — a position international law scholars widely criticised as legally inconsistent with UN Security Council referral doctrine.

ORD-08
Arms Embargo

Canada: New Arms License Suspension

Canada halted all new arms export permits to Israel in March 2024 following a vote in the House of Commons referencing the ICJ's January 26 provisional measures ruling. Prime Minister Justin Trudeau stated Canada would comply with ICC arrest warrant obligations. The Canadian government confirmed cessation of all arms shipments to Israel by January 2025. OHCHR welcomed the suspension as a model for Common Article 1 compliance.

ORD-09
Arms Embargo

Italy: Total Arms Export Halt

Italy imposed a complete cessation of new arms exports to Israel in October 2024 — moving beyond its earlier partial suspension — following a parliamentary committee review. OHCHR had specifically named Italy in its February 2024 call for immediate arms transfer cessation alongside Belgium, Spain, the Netherlands, and Japan. Italy's Foreign Minister Antonio Tajani simultaneously stated the arrest of Netanyahu was "unfeasible," drawing legal criticism from ICC member state compliance advocates.

ORD-10
Arms Embargo

Belgium: Flanders Arms Transit Ban

Belgium's Wallonia region had banned arms exports to Israel since 2009. In 2025, a Belgian court ruled that the Flanders region must also cease all military equipment transit to Israel — making Belgium the first country with a judicially enforced multi-regional arms embargo. Outgoing Prime Minister Alexander De Croo stated Belgium would "assume its responsibility" regarding the ICC arrest warrant, adding there can be "no double standards" in international law compliance.

ORD-11
Arms Embargo

Slovenia: Total Arms Trade Ban

Slovenia became the first EU member state to implement a comprehensive ban on all arms trades with Israel — including transit and imports — in August 2025. The ban was the most expansive single-state arms embargo imposed by any EU member state, covering both export and re-export routes. Slovenia also formally confirmed it would execute the ICC arrest warrant for Netanyahu if he entered Slovenian territory, joining Belgium, the Netherlands, Ireland, Lithuania, and Spain in that commitment.

ORD-12
Arms Embargo

Germany: Military Export Ban

Germany — historically Israel's second-largest arms supplier — announced a ban on further military equipment exports applicable to the Gaza Strip in August 2025. The decision came after sustained domestic legal pressure and a Düsseldorf administrative court ruling. Germany had initially said it would "examine" the ICC warrant, torn between its statutory commitment to ICC independence and its post-Holocaust political doctrine of unconditional support for Israel. OHCHR had repeatedly named Germany as one of the states most urgently required to act under Common Article 1 obligations.

ORD-13
Diplomatic Status

Ireland: Recognition of Statehood

Ireland officially recognised the State of Palestine on May 28, 2024, alongside Spain and Norway, describing it as an act of "historic justice." Taoiseach Simon Harris stated that Netanyahu would "absolutely be arrested" if he entered Ireland, reaffirming full compliance with the November 2024 ICC arrest warrant. Ireland also gave the strongest indication among EU states that it would enforce the warrant, and its foreign minister called on all EU members to follow in recognising Palestinian statehood.

ORD-14
Diplomatic Status

Norway: Recognition of Statehood

Norway officially recognised the State of Palestine on May 28, 2024. Prime Minister Jonas Gahr Store stated a two-state solution was "the only alternative" and that the recognition was an explicit contribution to making it politically and legally viable. Norway's recognition was coordinated with Ireland and Spain in a deliberate joint diplomatic signal to the European Union and United States. Israel recalled its ambassador from Oslo in response.

ORD-15
ICC Compliance

Lithuania: ICC Warrant Enforcement

Lithuania's Foreign Ministry formally confirmed it would execute the ICC arrest warrant for Benjamin Netanyahu and Yoav Gallant following the November 21, 2024 issuance. Lithuania was among the clearest and fastest confirmations in the EU — joining Belgium, the Netherlands, Ireland, Slovenia, and Spain in confirming full compliance with Rome Statute Article 89 arrest and surrender obligations, with no qualification or reservation regarding head-of-state immunity claims.

View Compliance Statement
ORD-16
Corporate Divestment

Japan (Itochu): Elbit Systems Severance

Japanese trading conglomerate Itochu Corporation — one of Japan's five largest general trading companies — terminated its collaboration with Israeli weapons manufacturer Elbit Systems in February 2024 at the explicit request of the Japanese Ministry of Defense. OHCHR specifically named Itochu's action in its February 22, 2024 statement as a model for the arms transfer obligations of all third-party states under IHL. Elbit Systems is one of the primary manufacturers of drones, surveillance systems, and artillery used in Gaza operations.

ORD-17
Non-Compliance

United States: Continued Arms Supply

The United States remains Israel's largest arms supplier by volume — responsible for the majority of Israel's military imports — and has increased arms transfers since October 7, 2023, despite growing UN, ICJ, and ICC legal pressure. OHCHR explicitly named the US alongside Germany as the states "most urgently" required to halt transfers. The Biden administration invoked a presidential waiver in May 2024 to override a State Department legal assessment finding a "reasonable" risk of IHL violations. The US also vetoed multiple UN Security Council ceasefire resolutions, providing diplomatic cover against binding international obligations.

ORD-18
Non-Compliance

Germany: Pre-2025 Arms Acceleration

Prior to its August 2025 export ban, Germany dramatically increased arms transfers to Israel following October 7 — approving €326.5 million in military exports in 2023, up from €32.4 million in 2022 (a tenfold increase). Germany's exports included tank parts, firearms, and military vehicles assessed by OHCHR experts as likely used in Gaza operations. Germany was specifically named by OHCHR in its February 2024 urgent call for immediate cessation of arms transfers, placing it among the states most directly implicated in third-state IHL complicity prior to its eventual 2025 position shift.
