A comprehensive, unredacted catalog of international resolutions determining the legal rights, territorial boundaries, and humanitarian obligations within the occupied territories.

194
11 DEC 1948 // UNGA

Right of Return

Resolves that refugees wishing to return to their homes and live at peace with their neighbors should be permitted to do so at the earliest practicable date, and that compensation should be paid for the property of those choosing not to return. The Palestinian refugee population — approximately 700,000 displaced in 1948 (the Nakba) — and their descendants, now estimated at 5.9 million registered with UNRWA, remain denied this right. Resolution 194 has been reaffirmed by the General Assembly annually for 75 consecutive years. Israel has never complied. The right of return is now recognized in customary international law independently of the resolution, per UNHCR and ICRC.

242
22 NOV 1967 // UNSC

Withdrawal from Occupied Territories

Emphasizes the "inadmissibility of the acquisition of territory by war" and mandates the withdrawal of Israeli armed forces from territories occupied in the 1967 Six-Day War — the West Bank, Gaza Strip, Sinai, and Golan Heights. It also affirms the necessity of "achieving a just settlement of the refugee problem." Israel has never withdrawn from the West Bank or Gaza in compliance with the resolution. The ICJ's July 2024 Advisory Opinion on the occupation explicitly invoked Res. 242 as part of the foundational legal matrix establishing the illegality of continued Israeli presence in Occupied Palestinian Territory. The resolution has been reaffirmed by Security Council resolutions 338 (1973), 1397 (2002), and ES-10/24 (2024).

2334
23 DEC 2016 // UNSC

Illegality of Settlements

Reaffirms that the establishment of settlements in the Palestinian territory occupied since 1967, including East Jerusalem, has no legal validity and constitutes a flagrant violation under international law and a major obstacle to achieving the two-state solution. Adopted 14–0 with one abstention (US). Calls on all states to distinguish, in their relevant dealings, between Israeli territory and the Occupied Palestinian Territory. The Trump administration subsequently refused to recognize the resolution. 700,000+ Israeli settlers now reside in the West Bank and East Jerusalem — the largest settler population since 1967. The ICJ Advisory Opinion (July 2024) directly extended this finding, classifying the entire settlement enterprise as illegal and calling for its dismantlement.

ES-10/24
18 SEP 2024 // UNGA

ICJ Advisory Opinion Enforcement

Adopted 124–14 with 43 abstentions. Demands an end to the unlawful Israeli presence in the Occupied Palestinian Territory within 12 months, acting on the International Court of Justice's July 19, 2024 Advisory Opinion which found the occupation illegal under international law. Requires Israel to cease all settlement activities, evacuate settlers, repeal discriminatory legislation, make reparations to Palestinians, and return confiscated property. Demands all UN member states cease aiding or assisting in maintaining the unlawful situation, and calls on international organizations to do the same. The 12-month compliance deadline passed in September 2025. Israel has not complied. The US voted against.

2728
25 MAR 2024 // UNSC

Immediate Ceasefire Mandate

United Nations Security Council resolution demanding an immediate ceasefire during the month of Ramadan, the unconditional release of all hostages, and the urgent lifting of all barriers to humanitarian aid provision — adopted 14–0 with the United States abstaining for the first time since October 7, 2023. The US abstention rather than veto represented a diplomatic break from its previous posture of blocking all ceasefire resolutions. Israel dismissed the resolution as "non-binding" — a characterization rejected by international law scholars and the UN Secretary-General. A ceasefire was not implemented. The resolution was followed by UNSC Res. 2735 (June 2024) and a subsequent US veto of a permanent ceasefire resolution in June 2025.

3236
22 NOV 1974 // UNGA

Inalienable Rights of Palestinians

Reaffirms the inalienable rights of the Palestinian people to self-determination without external interference, and the right to national independence and sovereignty, as well as the right of Palestinians to return to their homes and property. The resolution was adopted alongside Res. 3237, granting the PLO observer status at the UN — the first formal recognition of Palestinians as a distinct political entity with legal standing at the international level. It establishes a Committee on the Exercise of the Inalienable Rights of the Palestinian People, which continues to operate as a permanent UN body. The right to self-determination affirmed here has been repeatedly invoked in ICJ proceedings and ICC filings.

338
22 OCT 1973 // UNSC

Ceasefire and Implementation of 242

Calls upon all parties to the 1973 Yom Kippur/Ramadan War to cease all firing and terminate all military activity immediately, and to start immediately after the ceasefire the implementation of Security Council resolution 242 (1967) in all of its parts. Resolution 338 is paired with 242 as a tandem legal instrument — every subsequent resolution on the Palestinian question (1397, 2334, ES-10/24) reaffirms both resolutions as the foundational legal basis. The ICJ's separation wall advisory opinion (2004) cited 242 and 338 together as instruments whose implementation Israel is obligated to pursue in good faith. Israel has not implemented either resolution.

446
22 MAR 1979 // UNSC

Illegality of Territory Changes

Determines that the policy and practices of establishing settlements in the Palestinian and other Arab territories occupied since 1967 have no legal validity and constitute a serious obstruction to achieving a comprehensive peace. Calls on Israel, as the occupying Power, to abide scrupulously by the Geneva Conventions and to rescind its settlement policies. The resolution was passed 12–0 with the US, UK, and Norway abstaining. Resolution 446 was reinforced by UNSC 452 (1979), 465 (1980), 471 (1980), and 2334 (2016) — the cumulative body of law creating the most consistently reiterated prohibitions in the history of the Security Council. Israel has not complied with any of them. Settler population has grown from approximately 10,000 at the time of adoption to over 700,000 today.

67/19
29 NOV 2012 // UNGA

Non-member Observer State Status

The General Assembly grants Palestine non-member observer state status in the United Nations — adopted 138–9 with 41 abstentions — a pivotal diplomatic shift upgrading Palestine from "observer entity" (held since 1974) to "observer state." The reclassification allowed Palestine to accede to international legal instruments, including the Rome Statute of the ICC (acceded January 2015), enabling the ICC's jurisdiction over crimes committed in Palestinian territory. This created the formal legal pathway for the ICC investigation that resulted in the November 2024 arrest warrants for Netanyahu and Gallant. The resolution was subsequently reinforced by ES-10/23 (May 2024), which further upgraded Palestine's rights at the General Assembly.

37/43
03 DEC 1982 // UNGA

Legitimacy of National Liberation

Reaffirms the legitimacy of the struggle of peoples for independence, territorial integrity, national unity, and liberation from colonial and foreign domination and foreign occupation by all available means, including armed struggle. Adopted with broad support from the Non-Aligned Movement. The resolution is routinely invoked in academic and legal analysis distinguishing armed resistance by occupied peoples from terrorism under international law, a distinction central to debates about Hamas and Palestinian resistance groups in IHL scholarship. The ICRC and UN Special Rapporteurs have consistently held that the legality of resistance acts must be assessed against IHL rules on means and methods of warfare — not the political legitimacy of the cause itself.

377 A (V)
03 NOV 1950 // UNGA

Uniting for Peace

Resolves that if the Security Council, because of lack of unanimity among its permanent members, fails to act where there appears to be a threat to the peace, breach of the peace, or act of aggression, the General Assembly shall consider the matter immediately and may recommend collective measures. Invoked to convene the Tenth Emergency Special Session (ES-10) on Palestine — first in 1997 and reactivated repeatedly since October 7, 2023. ES-10 has served as the primary mechanism for General Assembly action on Gaza, producing resolutions ES-10/21 (humanitarian ceasefire, October 2023), ES-10/22 (ceasefire demand, December 2023), ES-10/23 (upgraded Palestinian rights, May 2024), and ES-10/24 (ICJ Advisory Opinion enforcement, September 2024).

181 (II)
29 NOV 1947 // UNGA

The Partition Plan

Recommends the partition of Mandatory Palestine into independent Arab and Jewish States, with a Special International Regime for the City of Jerusalem administered by the United Nations as a corpus separatum. Allocated approximately 56% of Mandatory Palestine to the Jewish state (despite the Jewish population owning approximately 7% of the land) and 43% to the Arab state. The Arab Higher Committee rejected the plan; it was accepted by the Jewish Agency. The British Mandate expired May 14, 1948; Israel declared independence and immediately expanded beyond the partition lines in the subsequent war. The corpus separatum status of Jerusalem has never been implemented. The resolution's territorial provisions have never governed on the ground — though it remains the foundational UN recognition of partition as a legal framework.

478
20 AUG 1980 // UNSC

Nullification of Jerusalem Law

Censures in the strongest terms the enactment of the "Basic Law: Jerusalem Capital of Israel" and determines that all legislative and administrative measures altering the character, status, and demographic composition of Jerusalem are null and void and must be rescinded. Calls upon states with diplomatic missions in Jerusalem to withdraw them — resulting in the transfer of all 13 existing embassies from Jerusalem. Adopted 14–0 with one US abstention. In 2017, the Trump administration recognized Jerusalem as Israel's capital and transferred the US embassy. UNGA Res. ES-10/L.22 (December 2017) subsequently demanded the decision be rescinded, adopted 128–9. As of 2026, the US and a handful of states maintain embassies in Jerusalem in violation of Res. 478.

ES-10/15
20 JUL 2004 // UNGA

Illegality of the Separation Wall

Acknowledges the ICJ Advisory Opinion of July 9, 2004 — adopted by a 14–1 majority of the Court — finding that the construction of the wall in the Occupied Palestinian Territory, including in and around East Jerusalem, is contrary to international law; that Israel is obligated to stop construction, dismantle existing sections, and make reparations. The General Assembly demanded Israel comply and called on all states not to recognize the illegal situation created by the wall. The wall — now approximately 700 km in length, 85% constructed inside the West Bank rather than on the Green Line — has never been dismantled. The ICJ's July 2024 Advisory Opinion on the occupation revisited the wall, affirming its illegality and calling for its dismantlement as part of ending the broader illegal occupation.

1860
08 JAN 2009 // UNSC

Gaza Ceasefire (Operation Cast Lead)

Calls for an immediate, durable and fully respected ceasefire leading to the full withdrawal of Israeli military forces from Gaza, and calls for unimpeded provision and distribution throughout Gaza of humanitarian assistance, including food, fuel and medical treatment — adopted 14–0 with the United States abstaining. Operation Cast Lead killed approximately 1,400 Palestinians (UN estimate) and 13 Israelis. Israel did not withdraw its forces in compliance with the resolution's timeline. The Goldstone Report (UNHRC, September 2009) subsequently found that both Israeli forces and Palestinian armed groups had committed war crimes during the operation, with the gravest violations attributed to Israeli forces. The resolution established the baseline for the now decades-long pattern of UNSC ceasefire demands and Israeli non-compliance.

452
20 JUL 1979 // UNSC

Halt of Settlement Construction

Calls upon the Government and people of Israel to cease, on an urgent basis, the establishment, construction and planning of settlements in the Arab territories occupied since 1967, including Jerusalem, determining that the settlements have no legal validity and acknowledging that they violate the Fourth Geneva Convention. Adopted 14–0 with the US abstaining. Resolution 452 directly complemented Res. 446 (adopted the same year) and was followed by Res. 465 (1980), which went further in calling on all states not to provide Israel with assistance to continue settlement activities. None of these resolutions produced Israeli compliance. The total number of Israeli settlers in occupied territories has grown from approximately 25,000 in 1979 to over 700,000 by 2024.

799
18 DEC 1992 // UNSC

Deportation Repatriation

Strongly condemns the action of the Israeli government to deport hundreds of Palestinian civilians — 415 Palestinians were deported to the Marj al-Zuhour area of Lebanon on December 17, 1992, following the killing of an Israeli border policeman — demanding their safe and immediate return to the occupied territories. The deportees, many of whom were Hamas and Islamic Jihad members, were stranded in freezing conditions in no-man's land between Israeli and Lebanese military lines for months. The mass deportation constituted a grave breach of the Fourth Geneva Convention under Art. 49(1). Israel eventually allowed the deportees to return in stages. The event marked a turning point in international recognition of deportation as a systematic Israeli counter-insurgency tool.

904
18 MAR 1994 // UNSC

Hebron Massacre Condemnation

Strongly condemns the massacre committed by an Israeli settler — Baruch Goldstein — against Palestinian worshippers at the Cave of the Patriarchs mosque in Hebron on February 25, 1994, in which 29 Palestinians were killed and 125 wounded during Ramadan morning prayers. Calls for measures to guarantee the safety and protection of Palestinian civilians throughout occupied territory and demands the disarming of settlers. The resolution was adopted unanimously including by the US. The Shamgar Commission inquiry established by Israel criticized the IDF's failure to protect civilians but did not recommend criminal accountability for failure to prevent the massacre. Goldstein's tomb in Kiryat Arba became a pilgrimage site for far-right Israeli nationalists and was eventually demolished by order of Israeli authorities.

1397
12 MAR 2002 // UNSC

Two-State Vision

The first Security Council resolution to explicitly affirm a vision of "a region where two States, Israel and Palestine, live side by side within secure and recognized borders" — adopted unanimously. The resolution demands the immediate cessation of all acts of violence, including all acts of terror, provocation, incitement, and destruction. It was adopted during the Second Intifada, at a time of intense violence. The two-state framework it endorsed became the stated basis of US, EU, and UN diplomacy for two decades. The ICJ's July 2024 Advisory Opinion found that the settlement enterprise — now comprising 700,000+ settlers — has rendered the viable establishment of a Palestinian state "impracticable," calling into question whether the two-state framework embedded in Res. 1397 remains physically achievable on the ground.

2720
22 DEC 2023 // UNSC

Humanitarian Access Enforcement

Demands the parties allow and facilitate the use of all available routes to and throughout the entire Gaza Strip for the provision of humanitarian assistance at scale, and requests the appointment of a Senior Humanitarian Coordinator for Gaza — adopted with the US abstaining. The US had previously vetoed an October 18, 2023 ceasefire resolution and abstained on this measure in lieu of a further veto. A Senior Humanitarian Coordinator was appointed. Israel did not open all available crossing routes. WFP and OCHA documented continued systematic blocking of aid convoys throughout 2024. The ICJ's January 26, 2024 provisional measures order referenced the ongoing humanitarian catastrophe as evidence supporting the prima facie plausibility of genocide and required Israel to ensure unimpeded humanitarian access as a provisional measure.

2735
10 JUN 2024 // UNSC

Three-Phase Ceasefire Framework

Adopted 14–0 with Russia abstaining. Endorses a comprehensive three-phase ceasefire proposal: Phase 1 — immediate full ceasefire, release of hostages (including women, elderly, and wounded), exchange of Palestinian prisoners, withdrawal of Israeli forces from populated areas, and large-scale humanitarian aid distribution. Phase 2 — release of all remaining living hostages, permanent end to hostilities, and full Israeli military withdrawal from Gaza. Phase 3 — multi-year reconstruction plan for Gaza. The resolution explicitly rejects "any attempt at demographic or territorial change in the Gaza Strip" and reaffirms support for a two-state solution. In January 2025, Israel and Hamas reached a ceasefire agreement heavily based on the Res. 2735 framework. A subsequent US veto in June 2025 blocked a Security Council resolution demanding implementation of Phase 2.

ES-10/23
10 MAY 2024 // UNGA

Upgraded Palestinian Rights & Membership Recommendation

Adopted 143–9 with 25 abstentions. Determines that the State of Palestine is qualified for full UN membership under Article 4 of the Charter and urges the Security Council to give favourable consideration to its membership application. Immediately upgrades Palestine's rights at the General Assembly — granting it the right to sit among member states in alphabetical order, participate in UN conferences, and present its candidacy to UN bodies. Stops short of full membership (which requires Security Council approval, subject to US veto). The US voted against. The vote was the largest international endorsement of Palestinian statehood in UN history. Ireland, Norway, and Spain formally recognised the State of Palestine 18 days later on May 28, 2024.

ES-10/21
27 OCT 2023 // UNGA

First Humanitarian Ceasefire Demand

The first General Assembly resolution on Gaza following October 7, 2023, calling for an immediate, durable and sustained humanitarian truce — adopted 120–14 with 45 abstentions. Demanded the protection of civilians, the lifting of the siege, and immediate humanitarian access. The resolution was invoked under the Uniting for Peace mechanism (Res. 377) after the US vetoed a Security Council ceasefire resolution on October 18, 2023. The US, Israel, and 12 others voted against. The vote triggered the series of ES-10 emergency sessions — ES-10/22 (December 2023), ES-10/23 (May 2024), and ES-10/24 (September 2024) — that have constituted the General Assembly's primary legislative response to the conflict.

465
01 MAR 1980 // UNSC

Third-State Settlement Complicity Ban

The strongest Security Council settlement resolution to date — calls on all states not to provide Israel with any assistance to be used specifically in connection with settlements in the occupied territories, and demands Israel dismantle existing settlements and cease all settlement activities. Adopted unanimously including by the US — the only unanimous settlement condemnation in UNSC history. The Carter administration subsequently disavowed the vote, claiming it was cast in error. Resolution 465 is the legal precedent most directly applicable to companies and states providing material support, planning services, finance, and equipment for settlement construction — including construction firms, banks, and tech companies currently listed in the UN database of businesses operating in settlements (the UN "blacklist" published under HRC Res. 31/36).

3237
22 NOV 1974 // UNGA

PLO Observer Status

Invites the Palestine Liberation Organization to participate in sessions and work of the General Assembly in the capacity of observer, marking the first formal international recognition of the PLO as the representative of the Palestinian people with legal standing at the United Nations. Adopted alongside Res. 3236. The grant of observer status was based on the General Assembly's determination that the Palestinian people — represented by the PLO — were a distinct national people with inalienable rights, independently of any state claim. This foundational recognition provided the legal basis for the subsequent upgrade to "observer entity" (1988), "observer state" (2012/Res. 67/19), and the further rights upgrade of ES-10/23 (2024).

2334
US VETO RECORD

US Security Council Veto Pattern

The United States has used its Security Council veto to block resolutions on Palestine on over 45 occasions since 1972 — more than any other state on a single issue in UN history. Notable vetoes since October 7, 2023: October 18, 2023 (Brazilian-drafted humanitarian ceasefire); December 8, 2023 (humanitarian ceasefire, 13–1); February 20, 2024 (Algerian-drafted ceasefire, 13–1–1); June 2025 (permanent ceasefire demanding Phase 2 of Res. 2735 implementation). Each veto prevented a binding Security Council ceasefire order under Chapter VII of the UN Charter — which would have carried legal enforcement mechanisms unavailable to General Assembly resolutions. Legal scholars at the Jurist, Al-Haq, and OHCHR have characterized the pattern of vetoes as itself a form of third-state complicity in IHL violations under Common Article 1 of the Geneva Conventions.
